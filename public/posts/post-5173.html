The Connection Between Baptism and the Creed (Romans 10:9–10)

2025-01-27

Baptism and the Creed

The sacrament of Baptism and the profession of faith in the Creed are intimately connected in Catholic tradition. This connection is beautifully expressed in Romans 10:9–10: "If you confess with your lips that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. For one believes with the heart and so is justified, and one confesses with the mouth and so is saved." Through Baptism, we make this profession of faith and are saved through our belief and confession.

The Creed is not just a statement of beliefs but is the faith that we profess at Baptism and that we are called to live throughout our lives. The connection between Baptism and the Creed shows that becoming a Christian involves both believing in the truth and professing that truth publicly.

The Baptismal Profession of Faith

During the celebration of Baptism, the person being baptized (or their godparents, in the case of infants) professes faith in the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. This profession is not just a formality but is the essential act that makes Baptism effective. Without this profession of faith, Baptism would be incomplete.

This profession of faith is made in response to three questions: "Do you believe in God, the Father almighty, creator of heaven and earth?" "Do you believe in Jesus Christ, his only Son, our Lord, who was born of the Virgin Mary, was crucified, died and was buried, rose from the dead and is seated at the right hand of the Father?" "Do you believe in the Holy Spirit, the holy Catholic Church, the communion of saints, the forgiveness of sins, the resurrection of the body, and life everlasting?"

The Apostles' Creed

The Apostles' Creed is the traditional creed used in Baptism. This creed is called "Apostles'" not because it was written by the apostles but because it contains the essential teachings that the apostles preached. The creed is a summary of the faith that has been passed down from the apostles to the present day.

The Apostles' Creed is divided into three parts, corresponding to the three persons of the Trinity. The first part professes faith in God the Father and His work of creation. The second part professes faith in Jesus Christ and His work of redemption. The third part professes faith in the Holy Spirit and His work of sanctification.

The Nicene Creed

The Nicene Creed is a more detailed version of the Apostles' Creed that was developed to address specific heresies about the nature of Christ and the Trinity. While the Apostles' Creed is typically used in Baptism, the Nicene Creed is used in the Sunday Mass and other important liturgies.

The Nicene Creed emphasizes the divinity of Christ and the equality of the three persons of the Trinity. It also includes more detailed statements about the Church, the sacraments, and the end times. This creed helps us to understand more deeply the faith that we profess at Baptism.

The Creed as a Rule of Faith

The Creed serves as a "rule of faith" - a standard by which we can measure our beliefs and ensure that they are in line with the apostolic tradition. The Creed is not just a list of beliefs but is a summary of the entire Christian faith. It helps us to understand what we believe and why we believe it.

This rule of faith is particularly important in today's world, where there are many different beliefs and opinions about God and religion. The Creed helps us to stay faithful to the truth that has been passed down from the apostles and to avoid being led astray by false teachings.

The Creed and Evangelization

The Creed is also an important tool for evangelization. When we profess the Creed, we are not just stating what we believe but are also witnessing to others about the truth of the Christian faith. The Creed is a powerful proclamation of the Gospel that can help others to come to know Christ.

This evangelizing aspect of the Creed is particularly important in today's secular culture. When we profess the Creed publicly, we are making a statement about what we believe and why it matters. This public profession can be a powerful witness to others.

The Creed and Unity

The Creed is also a sign of unity among Christians. When we profess the same Creed, we are acknowledging that we share the same faith and are members of the same Church. The Creed helps to unite us with other Christians throughout the world and throughout history.

This unity is particularly important in today's divided world. The Creed reminds us that we are part of a larger community of believers that transcends national, cultural, and linguistic boundaries. We are united with all those who profess the same faith.

The Creed and Prayer

The Creed is also a form of prayer. When we profess the Creed, we are not just stating facts but are praising God for who He is and what He has done. The Creed is a prayer of thanksgiving and adoration that helps us to grow in our relationship with God.

This prayerful aspect of the Creed is particularly evident when it is sung or recited in the liturgy. The Creed becomes a hymn of praise that helps us to worship God with our whole being - mind, heart, and voice.

The Creed and Discipleship

The Creed is also a guide for discipleship. The beliefs that we profess in the Creed should shape how we live our lives. If we believe in God the Father, we should trust in His providence. If we believe in Jesus Christ, we should follow His example. If we believe in the Holy Spirit, we should be open to His guidance.

This connection between belief and action is essential for authentic Christian discipleship. The Creed is not just something we say but is something we live. Our lives should reflect the beliefs that we profess.

Conclusion: A Living Faith

The connection between Baptism and the Creed shows that our faith is not just a private belief but is a public profession that shapes our entire lives. The Creed is the faith that we profess at Baptism and that we are called to live throughout our lives.

As we profess the Creed, let us remember that we are not just stating what we believe but are committing ourselves to live according to that belief. Let us also remember that the Creed is a gift from God that helps us to understand and live our faith more fully.
