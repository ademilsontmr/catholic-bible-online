The Divine Law Given to Moses

The Ten Commandments, given to Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 20, Deuteronomy 5), are fundamental to Catholic moral teaching. They represent God's revealed will for human conduct and establish the foundation for a just and holy society. Catholic tradition sees them not as arbitrary rules but as expressions of God's love and wisdom for human flourishing.

The Structure and Numbering

Catholic tradition follows the numbering established by St. Augustine, which differs slightly from Protestant and Jewish traditions. The Catholic numbering combines the prohibition of other gods and graven images as the first commandment, while separating the coveting of wife and goods into the ninth and tenth commandments. This structure emphasizes the unity of worship and the distinct nature of different types of coveting.

The First Three Commandments: Love of God

The first three commandments focus on our relationship with God. The first commandment calls us to worship God alone, rejecting idolatry in all its forms. The second commandment protects God's name and calls us to reverence in speech and action. The third commandment establishes the Sabbath as a day of rest and worship, pointing toward our ultimate rest in God.

The Fourth Commandment: Family and Authority

The fourth commandment, 'Honor your father and mother,' extends beyond biological parents to all legitimate authority. Catholic teaching emphasizes that this commandment establishes the foundation for all social relationships and calls us to respect the natural order established by God. It also implies the responsibility of parents to care for their children.

Commandments Five Through Seven: Respect for Life and Integrity

The fifth commandment, 'You shall not kill,' protects human life from conception to natural death. The sixth commandment protects the sacredness of human sexuality and marriage. The seventh commandment protects private property and calls for justice in economic relationships. These commandments establish the foundation for a just society.

The Eighth and Ninth Commandments: Truth and Purity

The eighth commandment, 'You shall not bear false witness,' calls us to truthfulness in all our relationships. The ninth commandment, 'You shall not covet your neighbor's wife,' protects the sanctity of marriage and calls us to purity of heart. These commandments address the interior dispositions that lead to sinful actions.

The Tenth Commandment: Contentment

The tenth commandment, 'You shall not covet your neighbor's goods,' calls us to contentment and trust in God's providence. It addresses the root cause of many sins: the disordered desire for what belongs to others. This commandment calls us to gratitude and detachment from material possessions.

The Commandments and the New Testament

Jesus does not abolish the commandments but fulfills them by revealing their deeper meaning. In the Sermon on the Mount, he calls his followers to go beyond external observance to interior conversion. The commandments are not just rules to follow but paths to holiness and union with God.

Contemporary Applications

The commandments remain relevant in our modern world, addressing issues like abortion, euthanasia, sexual morality, social media integrity, and economic justice. Catholic teaching applies the commandments to contemporary situations while preserving their essential meaning and purpose.

Grace and the Commandments

Catholic teaching emphasizes that keeping the commandments requires God's grace. The commandments reveal our need for God's help and point us toward the sacraments, prayer, and the life of virtue. They are not burdensome obligations but gifts that lead to true freedom and happiness.
