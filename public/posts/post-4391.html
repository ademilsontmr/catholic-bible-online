What the Catholic Church Teaches About the Real Presence

The Catholic Church teaches that in the Eucharist, the bread and wine are truly transformed into the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ, while retaining the appearance of bread and wine. Understanding this teaching helps us appreciate the mystery of the Eucharist and the ways in which God works through the sacraments to bring us into communion with Himself.

The Catholic Church teaches that when the priest, acting in the person of Christ, says the words of consecration over the bread and wine, these elements are truly transformed into the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ. This transformation is not symbolic or metaphorical but is real and substantial. The bread and wine become the actual Body and Blood of Christ, while retaining the appearance, taste, and other physical properties of bread and wine.

The Catholic Church teaches that this transformation is called transubstantiation, which means that the substance of the bread and wine is changed into the substance of Christ's Body and Blood, while the accidents (appearance, taste, etc.) remain those of bread and wine. This teaching emphasizes that the change is real and substantial, not just symbolic or metaphorical.

The Catholic Church teaches that the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist is not limited to the moment of reception but continues as long as the consecrated elements exist. This means that the Eucharist can be reserved in the tabernacle and adored, and that it remains the Body and Blood of Christ even when it is not being consumed. This teaching emphasizes the ongoing presence of Christ in the Eucharist.

The Catholic Church teaches that the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist is the same Christ who was born of the Virgin Mary, who suffered and died on the cross, who rose from the dead, and who ascended into heaven. This means that when we receive the Eucharist, we are truly receiving the same Christ who walked the earth and who offers us His love and grace.

These teachings demonstrate that the Catholic Church understands the Eucharist as a real and substantial encounter with Christ, not just a symbolic or metaphorical one.
