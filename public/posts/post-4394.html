What Is Transubstantiation?

Transubstantiation is the theological term that describes the change that occurs in the Eucharist when the bread and wine are consecrated by the priest. Understanding what transubstantiation means helps us appreciate the ways in which the Church explains this great mystery and the various philosophical and theological concepts that are used to articulate this teaching.

Transubstantiation means that the substance of the bread and wine is changed into the substance of Christ's Body and Blood, while the accidents (appearance, taste, smell, etc.) remain those of bread and wine. The term 'substance' refers to what something really is at its deepest level, while the term 'accidents' refers to the observable properties of something. In the case of the Eucharist, the substance (what the bread and wine really are) is changed into the substance of Christ's Body and Blood, while the accidents (what we can see, taste, and touch) remain those of bread and wine.

Transubstantiation is not a physical or chemical change but is a metaphysical change that occurs at the level of being. The bread and wine do not change in their physical properties, but they do change in what they really are. This change is brought about by the power of God working through the words of consecration spoken by the priest, who acts in the person of Christ.

Transubstantiation is a unique and mysterious change that has no parallel in the natural world. It is not like other changes that we observe in nature, such as the change of water into ice or the change of a caterpillar into a butterfly. It is a supernatural change that is brought about by the power of God and that is beyond our ability to fully understand or explain.

Transubstantiation is a change that is complete and total. The bread and wine are completely transformed into the Body and Blood of Christ, so that there is no bread or wine left, only the Body and Blood of Christ. This complete transformation means that when we receive the Eucharist, we are truly receiving Christ Himself, not just a symbol or representation of Him.

These aspects of transubstantiation help us understand what this theological term means and how it explains the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist.
