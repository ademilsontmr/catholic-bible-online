Matthew: The First Gospel

The Gospel of Matthew, traditionally placed first in the New Testament canon, was written primarily for a Jewish-Christian audience. Catholic tradition attributes this Gospel to Matthew, one of the twelve apostles and a former tax collector. The Gospel's structure, themes, and theological focus make it particularly important for understanding the continuity between the Old and New Testaments and the establishment of the Church.

The Genealogy and Fulfillment

Matthew begins with a genealogy that traces Jesus' lineage from Abraham through David to Joseph, emphasizing Jesus' messianic credentials and His fulfillment of Old Testament promises. The genealogy is structured in three groups of fourteen generations, highlighting key moments in salvation history. Catholic interpretation sees this as establishing Jesus as the fulfillment of God's promises to Abraham and David.

The Sermon on the Mount

The Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) is the most comprehensive collection of Jesus' teachings in the New Testament. Catholic tradition sees this as the new law that fulfills and perfects the Old Testament law. The Beatitudes, the Lord's Prayer, and the teachings on love of enemies provide the foundation for Christian morality and spirituality. The sermon emphasizes interior conversion and the call to holiness.

Matthew's Use of the Old Testament

Matthew frequently uses the formula 'This was to fulfill what was spoken by the prophet' to show how Jesus fulfills Old Testament prophecies. This emphasis on fulfillment is central to Catholic understanding of the unity of Scripture and God's plan of salvation. Matthew's approach helps Catholics see the Old Testament as preparation for Christ and the New Testament as the fulfillment of God's promises.

The Kingdom of Heaven

Matthew uses the phrase 'kingdom of heaven' (rather than 'kingdom of God') throughout his Gospel, reflecting Jewish sensitivity about using God's name. Catholic teaching sees the kingdom as both present and future - inaugurated by Christ but to be fully realized at the end of time. The parables of the kingdom reveal its nature and the conditions for entering it.

Peter and the Church

Matthew's Gospel contains the famous passage where Jesus gives Peter the keys to the kingdom and establishes him as the rock on which the Church will be built (16:18-19). Catholic tradition sees this as establishing the papacy and the hierarchical structure of the Church. The authority given to Peter and the apostles is seen as continuing in the Church's teaching office.

The Great Commission

Matthew ends with the Great Commission (28:19-20), where Jesus sends the apostles to make disciples of all nations, baptizing them and teaching them to observe all His commands. Catholic teaching sees this as establishing the Church's missionary mandate and the importance of both baptism and catechesis. The promise of Jesus' presence until the end of the age assures the Church of divine guidance.

Matthew's Portrait of Jesus

Matthew presents Jesus as the new Moses, the teacher of the new law, and the Emmanuel (God with us). This portrait emphasizes Jesus' authority as a teacher and His role in establishing the new covenant. Catholic tradition sees this as supporting the Church's understanding of Jesus as both divine and human, the perfect teacher and the perfect sacrifice.

The Eucharist in Matthew

While Matthew's account of the Last Supper is similar to the other Synoptics, his emphasis on Jesus' words about the bread and wine as His body and blood supports Catholic teaching on the Real Presence. The institution narrative in Matthew 26:26-29 provides the scriptural foundation for the Church's understanding of the Eucharist as the new covenant meal.

Contemporary Relevance

Matthew's Gospel remains relevant for modern Catholics because it addresses fundamental questions about discipleship, morality, and the Church's mission. The emphasis on both faith and works, the call to holiness, and the importance of the Church community provide guidance for contemporary Christian life. Matthew's approach to Scripture helps Catholics understand the relationship between the Old and New Testaments.
