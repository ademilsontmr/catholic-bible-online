The Catholic View of Baptism in the Didache (Matthew 28:19)

2025-01-27

The Didache and Early Christian Baptism

The Didache, also known as "The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles," is one of the most important early Christian documents, dating from the late first or early second century. This ancient text provides valuable insights into how the early Church understood and practiced Baptism. The Didache's instructions on Baptism show that the Church took this sacrament very seriously and had specific requirements for its celebration.

The Didache begins its section on Baptism by quoting Jesus' command from Matthew 28:19: "Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit." This shows that the early Church saw Baptism as essential to the mission of making disciples.

The Didache's Baptismal Instructions

The Didache provides detailed instructions for how Baptism should be administered. It states: "Concerning baptism, baptize in this way: Having first said all these things, baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, in living water. But if you have no living water, baptize in other water; and if you cannot do so in cold water, do so in warm. But if you have neither, pour out water three times upon the head in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit."

These instructions show several important aspects of early Christian Baptism. First, Baptism was to be administered in the name of the Trinity, following Jesus' command. Second, the preferred method was immersion in "living water" (running water, such as a river or stream). Third, the Church was flexible about the method of Baptism, allowing for pouring water over the head when immersion was not possible.

The Preparation for Baptism

The Didache emphasizes the importance of preparation for Baptism. Before baptizing, the minister was to "say all these things," referring to the moral and doctrinal instruction that preceded Baptism. This shows that the early Church had a catechumenate (period of instruction) for those preparing for Baptism.

This preparation included instruction in the "Two Ways" - the way of life and the way of death. The way of life involved love of God and neighbor, while the way of death involved various sins and vices. This moral formation was essential for those preparing to receive Baptism.

The Trinitarian Formula

The Didache's insistence on baptizing "in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" shows that the early Church understood Baptism as a Trinitarian sacrament. This formula was not just a formality but expressed the Church's belief that Baptism involves the work of all three persons of the Trinity.

This Trinitarian understanding of Baptism is consistent with the New Testament and shows that the early Church had a developed understanding of the Trinity. The Didache's use of this formula also shows that the Church was careful to follow the exact words of Jesus' command.

The Flexibility in Method

The Didache's instructions show that the early Church was flexible about the method of Baptism while being strict about the essential elements. While immersion in living water was preferred, the Church allowed for other methods when necessary. This flexibility shows the Church's pastoral concern for making Baptism available to all who desired it.

This flexibility also shows that the Church understood that the essential element of Baptism is not the method but the grace conferred through the sacrament. The Church was more concerned with ensuring that people received the grace of Baptism than with insisting on a particular method.

The Connection to the Eucharist

The Didache shows that Baptism was closely connected to the Eucharist. After the instructions on Baptism, the document immediately moves to instructions on the Eucharist. This connection shows that Baptism was understood as the gateway to full participation in the Christian community, including the celebration of the Eucharist.

This connection between Baptism and the Eucharist is consistent with the New Testament and shows that the early Church understood these two sacraments as the foundation of Christian initiation. Baptism made one a member of the Church, and the Eucharist sustained and nourished that membership.

The Moral Requirements

The Didache's instructions on Baptism include moral requirements for those preparing for the sacrament. The document lists various sins that must be avoided, including murder, adultery, fornication, theft, and false witness. This shows that the early Church saw Baptism as requiring a commitment to live according to Christian moral standards.

These moral requirements were not seen as conditions for receiving God's grace but as the natural response to that grace. The early Church understood that those who received the grace of Baptism should live lives that reflected their new identity in Christ.

The Role of Fasting

The Didache mentions that both the one being baptized and the minister should fast before Baptism. This practice shows that the early Church saw Baptism as a solemn and sacred event that required spiritual preparation. Fasting was a way of preparing oneself to receive the grace of the sacrament.

This practice of fasting before Baptism also shows the Church's understanding that the sacraments are not magical rites but require the active participation and preparation of those involved. The Church wanted to ensure that people were spiritually prepared to receive the grace of Baptism.

The Community Aspect

The Didache's instructions on Baptism show that this sacrament was not a private affair but involved the entire Christian community. The document mentions that others should also fast before the baptism, showing that the community was expected to support and pray for those being baptized.

This community aspect of Baptism shows that the early Church understood that becoming a Christian was not just an individual decision but involved joining a community of believers. The Church saw itself as responsible for helping new members grow in their faith.

Conclusion: A Living Tradition

The Didache's instructions on Baptism show that the early Church had a well-developed understanding and practice of this sacrament. The document reveals that Baptism was seen as essential for Christian initiation, requiring proper preparation, and involving the entire Christian community.

As we reflect on the Didache's teaching, we can see that the Catholic Church's current practice of Baptism is deeply rooted in the apostolic tradition. The early Church's understanding of Baptism as a Trinitarian sacrament that requires preparation and involves the community continues to inform the Church's practice today.
