Why Catholics Fast: A Complete Guide to the Spiritual Practice of Fasting

2025-01-27

Why Do Catholics Fast?

Fasting is an ancient spiritual practice that has been part of the Catholic tradition since the earliest days of the Church. Catholics fast for many reasons: to grow closer to God, to practice self-discipline, to express repentance, to prepare for important feasts, and to show solidarity with the poor. Fasting is not just about giving up food, but is a way of focusing our attention on God and growing in spiritual strength.

The Catholic Church teaches that fasting is a powerful spiritual tool that helps us to overcome our passions, to grow in virtue, and to become more like Christ. Jesus Himself fasted for 40 days in the desert before beginning His public ministry, and He taught His disciples about the importance of fasting. The Church continues this tradition, calling the faithful to fast on certain days and during certain seasons.

The Biblical Foundation

The practice of fasting has a strong biblical foundation. In the Old Testament, we see many examples of fasting: Moses fasted for 40 days on Mount Sinai (Exodus 34:28), Elijah fasted during his journey to Mount Horeb (1 Kings 19:8), and the people of Nineveh fasted in repentance (Jonah 3:5). Fasting was often associated with prayer, repentance, and seeking God's guidance.

In the New Testament, Jesus fasted for 40 days in the desert (Matthew 4:1-11), and He taught His disciples about fasting. In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus said, 'When you fast, do not look gloomy like the hypocrites' (Matthew 6:16), indicating that fasting was an expected practice for His followers. The early Church also practiced fasting, as we see in the Acts of the Apostles (Acts 13:2-3).

The Spiritual Benefits of Fasting

Fasting offers many spiritual benefits that help us to grow in holiness and to become more like Christ:

Self-Discipline: Fasting helps us to develop self-control and to overcome our passions. By denying ourselves food or other pleasures, we learn to control our desires and to focus on what is truly important.

Spiritual Focus: Fasting helps us to focus our attention on God and to grow in prayer. When we are not preoccupied with food or other pleasures, we can devote more time and energy to prayer and spiritual reading.

Repentance: Fasting is often associated with repentance and conversion. It helps us to recognize our sins and to turn away from them, seeking God's forgiveness and mercy.

Solidarity with the Poor: Fasting helps us to identify with those who are hungry and in need. It reminds us of our responsibility to help others and to work for justice and charity.

When Do Catholics Fast?

The Catholic Church has established certain days and seasons for fasting:

Ash Wednesday and Good Friday: These are the two main days of fasting in the Catholic Church. On these days, Catholics between the ages of 18 and 59 are required to fast, eating only one full meal and two smaller meals that together do not equal a full meal.

Fridays of Lent: During Lent, Catholics are required to abstain from meat on all Fridays. This is a form of penance and a reminder of Christ's sacrifice on Good Friday.

Other Fridays: Outside of Lent, Catholics are encouraged to practice some form of penance on Fridays, which may include fasting or abstaining from meat.

Ember Days: These are traditional days of fasting and prayer that occur four times a year, though they are no longer strictly required.

How to Fast Properly

Fasting should be done with the right intention and in the right way. Here are some guidelines for fasting properly:

Start Small: If you are new to fasting, start with small sacrifices and gradually increase them. You might begin by giving up a favorite food or drink for a day.

Focus on God: Fasting should always be done with the intention of growing closer to God. Use the time and energy you save from eating to pray, read Scripture, or perform acts of charity.

Be Discreet: Jesus taught us to fast in secret, not to draw attention to ourselves. Fasting is a personal spiritual practice, not a public display of piety.

Consider Your Health: Fasting should not endanger your health. If you have medical conditions that make fasting difficult, consult with your doctor and consider alternative forms of penance.

Fasting and Prayer

Fasting and prayer are closely connected in the Catholic tradition. Fasting helps to prepare our hearts for prayer and makes our prayers more effective. When we fast, we are more focused and attentive to God's voice, and our prayers become more sincere and heartfelt.

Fasting also helps us to grow in humility and to recognize our dependence on God. When we deny ourselves food, we are reminded that we need God more than we need food, and that He is the source of all our strength and nourishment.

Fasting and Almsgiving

Fasting is often practiced together with almsgiving (giving to the poor). When we fast, we save money that we would have spent on food, and we can use that money to help those in need. This practice helps us to grow in charity and to show solidarity with the poor.

Almsgiving also helps to ensure that our fasting is not just about self-improvement, but is about serving others and growing in love. It reminds us that our spiritual practices should always lead us to greater love for God and for our neighbor.

Fasting and the Saints

Many saints have practiced fasting as a way of growing in holiness. St. Francis of Assisi was known for his strict fasting, and St. Catherine of Siena practiced extreme fasting as a form of penance and prayer. The saints show us that fasting can be a powerful tool for spiritual growth when practiced with the right intention.

The saints also teach us that fasting should be balanced with other spiritual practices and should not become an obsession or a source of pride. Fasting is a means to an end (growing closer to God), not an end in itself.

Fasting in the Modern World

In our modern world, fasting can be particularly challenging due to the abundance of food and the constant availability of snacks and treats. However, this makes fasting even more valuable as a spiritual practice. Fasting helps us to resist the consumerism and materialism of our culture and to focus on what is truly important.

Fasting can also be adapted to modern circumstances. In addition to fasting from food, we can fast from other things that distract us from God, such as television, social media, or other forms of entertainment. These forms of fasting can be just as valuable for spiritual growth.

Conclusion: A Path to Holiness

Fasting is a powerful spiritual practice that helps us to grow closer to God and to become more like Christ. It teaches us self-discipline, helps us to focus on prayer, and reminds us of our dependence on God. Fasting also helps us to show solidarity with the poor and to grow in charity.

While fasting can be challenging, it is a practice that has been valued by the Church for centuries and has helped countless Christians to grow in holiness. By practicing fasting with the right intention and in the right way, we can experience its many spiritual benefits and grow closer to God.

Fasting is not just about giving up food, but is about growing in love for God and for others. It is a path to holiness and a way of becoming more like Christ, who fasted and prayed for our salvation.
